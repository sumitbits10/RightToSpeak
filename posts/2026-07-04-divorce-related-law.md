  Divorce in India


  Divorce is the legal process of ending a valid marriage through a court order. The grounds and procedure vary depending on the applicable law, but the most common laws include:


  Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 (for Hindus, Buddhists, Jains, and Sikhs)

  Special Marriage Act, 1954 (for civil marriages and interfaith marriages)

  Indian Divorce Act, 1869

  Dissolution of Muslim Marriages Act, 1939

  Relevant personal laws for other communities.

  Types of Divorce
---
1. Mutual Consent Divorce



Both husband and wife agree to end the marriage.

They jointly file a petition before the family court.

They settle issues relating to child custody, maintenance, alimony, and property.

This is generally the quickest and least contentious method.



2. Contested Divorce

One spouse files for divorce without the other's consent on legally recognized grounds.



Common Grounds for Divorce



Depending on the applicable law, common grounds include:



Cruelty (physical or mental)

Adultery

Desertion (abandonment for the legally prescribed period)

Conversion to another religion

Mental disorder of a serious nature

Communicable venereal disease (where applicable under the governing law)

Renunciation of the world

Presumption of death (person not heard of for seven years)

Other grounds provided under the applicable statute.

Important Legal Issues



During divorce proceedings, the court may decide:



Child custody and visitation rights

Maintenance/alimony for the spouse

Child maintenance

Division or settlement of property (subject to applicable law)

Return of dowry articles, gifts, or streedhan where legally applicable.

Jurisdiction



A divorce petition is generally filed before the appropriate Family Court or District Court having jurisdiction, such as where:



The marriage was solemnized,

The spouses last lived together, or

The respondent resides (subject to the governing law).

Documents Commonly Required

Marriage certificate (if available)

Identity and address proofs

Marriage photographs (if required)

Proof supporting the grounds for divorce (for contested cases)

Documents relating to children, income, and assets, where relevant.
